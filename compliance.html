Red lines — do not cross

1. No tipping-off — criminal offence (LCLDA Art. 44). Decline / close uses neutral grounds only; never reveal STR, freeze, AML reason.

2. No STR later than 24 h from determination (LCLDA Art. 24). 5 bd in Instructivo Art. 43 is the older outer envelope — statute prevails.

3. No UNSC / national list match without freeze + UIF report — FGR has max 24 h to issue instructions (LECAT Art. 37).

4. No VASP transfer without full Travel Rule 7-field data — even sub-$1,000 (Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 Reform 2).

5. No SDD on suspicion or higher-risk scenario (Instr. Art. 14 last par.). Suspicion overrides matrix.

6. No operation outside registered Art. 19 perimeter (LEAD Arts. 38-40).

7. No relationship without BO identified to a natural person — apply 10% as floor (Instr. Art. 20(b)).

8. No identity verification skipped — pre-relationship gate at $0 (Instr. Art. 12 last par. Reform 2). No verification → no relationship.

9. No file destroyed before 15 years from operation date / relationship termination (LCLDA Art. 26).

10. No PEP relationship without senior-management written approval (Instr. Art. 13(c) Reform 2). PEP definition + 5-year persistence: LCLDA Art. 19.

Risk-Based Approach

RBA & Customer Risk Matrix — Instr. Arts. 4 & 10

The Risk-Based Approach (RBA) is the foundation of every other AML obligation. Identify and understand ML/TF/FPWMD risks → allocate resources proportionally → document the rationale. The customer risk matrix operationalises the RBA: it weights inherent-risk criteria into a single classification (low / medium / high) that determines the DD level, the monitoring intensity and the refresh cadence.

Five RBA principles — Instr. Art. 4

1. Identify · Assess · Understand

Identify ML/TF/FPWMD risks; assess their materiality; understand how they manifest in the entity's business model. Resources allocated to ensure effective mitigation.

2. Proportionality

Measures adopted must be proportionate to identified risks. Higher risk → enhanced measures; lower risk → simplified measures permitted.

3. Document & justify

All risk assessments and the resulting measures must be justified and documented — both higher-risk escalations and lower-risk simplifications. Without documentation, no measure is defensible to CNAD/UIF.

4. SDD prohibition on suspicion

Simplified measures shall not be applied when there is a suspicion of ML/TF/FPWMD — regardless of the customer's general risk classification. Suspicion overrides the matrix.

5. Differentiated scope

Reporting Entities may differentiate the scope of measures depending on the type and level of risk for various risk factors. Per-factor calibration is encouraged.

FPWMD scope clarification

FPWMD risk per GAFI R.1 refers strictly and exclusively to possible non-compliance, failure to implement or evasion of targeted financial sanctions (GAFI R.7). Apply R.7 framework for FPWMD risk assessment.

Customer inherent-risk criteria — Instr. Art. 10 (a–g)

The Reporting Entity must determine the inherent risk level of each customer / counterparty by weighting risk ratings across the criteria below. Any single criterion can elevate the customer to high risk; the entity defines the weighting in the AML manual.

Statutory consequence — Art. 10 last paragraph. When the determination yields high inherent risk, the Reporting Entity must apply EDD measures in addition to Standard CDD per Art. 12. The EDD measures must be calibrated "in line with the activities, nature, size, operations and risk level of the Reporting Entity" — risk-based proportionality applies even within the EDD layer.

Safeguard — Instr. Art. 11. The mere request for information by UIF (without an accompanying operational unit of FGR) about a natural or legal person is NOT a criterion for determining high inherent risk. UIF inquiries are intelligence-gathering; they do not, by themselves, justify EDD escalation or relationship termination. Document the UIF request, cooperate, but do not auto-escalate the customer's risk profile on that basis alone.

DASPs registered directly under LEAD/RPSAD (CNAD-supervised) are not bound by BCR Standards Committee provisions but commonly use them as best-practice reference.

VASP-specific CDD framework

Chapter IV-B (Reform 1) layered onto the general regime

Chapter IV-B of the Instructivo (Arts. 84-A / 84-B / 84-C, added by Reform 1) does not set separate amount thresholds for VASPs. Instead it confirms that the general three-tier DD architecture (SDD / CDD / EDD) applies, and adds three VASP-specific obligations on top: own-platform transaction-limit policies, identity-verification gate before relationship (via Reform 2 of Art. 12), and the VASP Travel Rule anchor (Art. 84-B(1)(e)). What Chapter IV-B adds on top of the general regime

Art. 84-B(1)(a) — DD policies

VASPs must run a risk-based AML program with all three DD tiers — Simplified, Standard and Intensified — calibrated to activities, nature, size, operations and customer/transactional risk profile.

Confirms the three general DD tiers (Arts. 12 / 13 / 14 + LCLDA Arts. 15-17) apply to VASPs. No VASP-specific amount thresholds. Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(a) Reform 1
Art. 84-B(1)(b) — Transaction-limit policies

VASPs must adopt own-platform transaction-limit policies tied to CDD, risk-management and ML-prevention measures. The platform itself must enforce these limits operationally.

Lex specialis for VASPs. Each VASP publishes its own internal limits in the AML manual; they sit on top of the statutory floor. Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(b) Reform 1
Art. 84-B(1)(e) — Customer & operation records

VASPs must maintain customer and operation records sufficient to identify the origin and destination of virtual-asset transactions on the platform.

VASP-specific Travel Rule anchor; the data set is specified in Art. 52 par. 2 (Reform 2). Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(e) Reform 1
Art. 12 last par. (Reform 2) — Identity gate

Identity verification via official documents must be completed before the commercial relationship begins. If identity cannot be verified, the relationship cannot start, and the failure must be analysed under the suspicious-transaction framework.

Pre-relationship gate at $0, independent of CDD amount triggers. Applies to all reporting entities (incl. VASPs). Instr. Art. 12 Reform 2

Practical implication for the AML manual. Because Art. 84-B(1)(b) requires internal transaction-limit policies, the VASP cannot simply quote the statutory floor and stop there. The AML manual must publish concrete VASP-specific limits — typically calibrated at or below the statutory floor (internal trigger thresholds set per the entity's risk-based assessment) — and link each limit to the corresponding DD tier and monitoring intensity. CNAD reviews these internal limits as part of the AML manual sign-off during registration and ongoing supervision.

RPSAD Art. 22 — $125K monthly aggravator (VASP-specific)

Independent of the CDD/EDD trigger ladder, RPSAD imposes a VASP-specific aggravating-risk threshold on monthly capital / fund injections. Once a client crosses the threshold in a calendar month, increased surveillance and additional documentation are mandatory.

Trigger
  • ≥ US$125,000 in new capital / fund injections by a single client
  • Per cumulative calendar month (aggregate, not single transaction)
  • USD or equivalent in any other currency
  • Threshold may be reviewed by CNAD annually, or when client's operations change in nature / volume
RPSAD Art. 22
Mandatory measures on trigger
  • Increased surveillance by the VASP — KYT intensified, monitoring cadence reduced
  • Client explanation of the origin of funds — supported by documents (not declarative)
  • Electronic affidavit by client on the lawful origin of funds
  • Issuer identification for any DA involved — name, URL address, contact
  • Document the trigger and the response in the client file (CDD case)
Operates as an aggravator on top of the existing DD level — does not replace SDD/CDD/EDD selection but adds documentary requirements for that month's flow.

How $125K interacts with the CDD framework. The Art. 22 threshold operates per calendar month on a single client's cumulative inflows. It is not a CDD-level trigger — a high-volume client may already be on Standard CDD (ongoing relationship) or EDD (PEP / risk factor), but crossing $125K in a month adds the documentary obligations above. For the AML manual: include $125K as an automatic monthly review trigger in the KYT escalation matrix, distinct from the $1,000 / $10K / $25K reporting / CDD thresholds.

DASP-specific DDC threshold cascade — UIF Acuerdo 734/2022

In addition to the general LCLDA Art. 15 framework, the UIF DASP Guide (Acuerdo No. 734, in force since 2 December 2022) imposes a per-amount DDC level cascade specifically for VASP transactions. This cascade does not replace the risk-based selection — it sets a floor per amount that overlays the general framework.

Operation amountDDC level (DASP mandatory)What this means
≤ US$1,000 Simplificada (SDD) Lighter scope — basic identification + sanctions / PEP screen + Travel Rule data. SDD not available on suspicion or higher-risk scenario.
US$1,000 – US$4,000 Estándar (Standard CDD) Full Art. 12 set: identification + verification + BO chain + risk profile + SoF declared.
> US$4,000 Intensificada (EDD) Automatic EDD by amount alone — no risk factor required. Senior-management written approval + documentary SoF + SoW + enhanced monitoring + KYT every transaction.

Override rules — risk + suspicion always escalate. The amount-based cascade is a floor, not a ceiling. Any of the following overrides the cascade upward regardless of amount:

  • PEP / high-risk jurisdiction / opaque ownership / structuring / flagged wallet / 3rd-party funder / adverse media / cash-intensive → EDD per Art. 13 Reform 2 (regardless of amount, even if < $1,000)
  • Any suspicion of ML/TF/FPWMD → full CDD + STR pipeline, SDD not acceptable per Art. 14 last par.
  • $125K cumulative monthly aggravator (RPSAD Art. 22) → adds documentary SoF + affidavit + DA-issuer ID on top of base DD level

Practical decision logic: apply the stricter of (i) Acuerdo 734 amount cascade, (ii) Art. 13 risk-factor escalation, (iii) Art. 22 aggravator. The wizard applies all three.

Why this matters operationally. A US$4,500 occasional e-transfer with a low-risk client and no risk factor → under the general Art. 15(b) chain alone, this would be Standard CDD. Under Acuerdo 734 § V.2.b.II, it is automatically EDD (intensificada). The DASP-specific cascade is stricter than the general regime for amounts > US$4,000. CNAD / UIF supervisors apply Acuerdo 734 during inspection.

Identification & verification

Identity verification — the gating step

Reform 2 of the Instructivo added a binding sequence: identity must be verified BEFORE the commercial relationship begins, via official identification documents issued by competent public authorities. If verification cannot be completed, the relationship cannot start — and the failure is itself analyzed under the suspicious-transaction framework.

Natural person — minimum data set

Instr. Art. 12(a) + identity-verification clause (Reform 2)

  • Full name as on official document
  • Type and number of ID (DUI, passport, residence card)
  • Date of birth
  • Address (utility bill (recent) / bank statement / registry)
  • Occupation (real economic activity, not just DUI/NIT data)
  • Tax ID (NIT) if applicable
  • PEP self-declaration + screening
Verification before relationship: official document must be current at onboarding. Instr. Art. 12(a) Reform 2
Legal entity — minimum data set

Instr. Art. 12 + Reform 2 identity clause + Art. 20 BO chain

  • Corporate name + commercial name
  • Principal address in country
  • Articles of incorporation + amendments — registered with the Commercial Register
  • Powers binding the legal person + senior management names
  • NIT + operating licence
  • Nature of main activity (real, beyond registry classification)
  • Beneficial owner chain to natural person (≥10% floor)
  • Authorised signatories with IDs and powers
Identity must be verified BEFORE relationship begins. Instr. Art. 12(a) Reform 2

Method & channel

  • In person OR remote via digital / electronic channels, after a documented risk analysis of the chosen channel Instr. Art. 12(a)
  • The identification document must be current at the time of onboarding
  • The economic activity declared on the DUI / NIT is not sufficient — further enquiry is required to determine the actual activity in the real economy
  • For legal persons: identification reaches the ownership structure and ultimate beneficial owner, not just the corporate counterparty
  • Trustees must disclose their status when establishing a relationship; identification of all parties to the trust is required Instr. Arts. 21, 21-A

If identity cannot be verified. The commercial relationship may not be commenced. The Reporting Entity must analyze the circumstances under the unusual / suspicious framework (Instructivo Title II Chapters VII–VIII) and consider filing an attempted suspicious-transaction report. Instr. Art. 12 Reform 2 · Instr. Art. 47

Once identity is verified, build a customer profile via interview sheet and a sworn statement, defining the economic activity. The sworn statement is per customer, not per product. Instr. Art. 12(b)

Beneficial owner

Resolve to a natural person — apply 10% as floor

El Salvador uses a stricter-of-three approach for beneficial ownership. Always identify and verify the natural person at the top of the chain. UBO must be identified before the relationship begins or any operation is executed.

Three statutory UBO definitions

10% participation floor

Operational baseline (stricter rule)

Natural person who directly or indirectly owns or controls ≥10% of capital or voting rights of a legal person, OR exercises direct/indirect control by other means of management.

Instr. Arts. 12(a), 20(b)
25% statutory threshold

LCLDA floor — Instructivo overrides stricter

The AML Law uses 25% as the participation threshold for legal persons. The Instructivo lowers it to 10% for operational rigour. Apply the stricter (10%) per the stricter rule.

LCLDA Art. 15 par. 5
Majority + control catch-all

Reform 2 cascade

Identify natural person(s) holding majority shareholding. If doubt about majority being UBO, OR no natural person controls via shareholding → identify control by other means. If still impossible → identify highest-ranking management officer.

Instr. Art. 21-A Reform 2

UBO concept also covers nominee structures

Independent of the % thresholds, Art. 20(a) defines as UBO the natural person on whose behalf the relationship is sought or the operation is conducted. This catches nominee / fronting structures where the legal customer is acting for an undisclosed beneficiary.

  • Acting-on-own-behalf duty. Reporting Entities must collect information from customers / counterparties to determine whether they act on their own behalf or on behalf of third parties. Where there are indications or certainty that they are not acting on their own behalf, the entity must collect precise information to identify the person on whose behalf they act. Instr. Art. 20
  • Pre-relationship obligation. Customers must provide UBO information prior to establishing business relationships or executing any operation. Instr. Art. 20

Listed-company exception. When the customer (or the holder of ≥10% / ≥25% participation in the customer) is a commercial company listed on a stock exchange, subject to information-disclosure requirements and adequate UBO transparency, identification of UBO of those companies is not required. Document the exemption with proof of listing and disclosure-regime applicability. Instr. Art. 20(b) · LCLDA Art. 15 par. 6

Trusts and other legal arrangements. Identify all parties: settlor and whoever controls the settlor; trustee(s); protector (if any); beneficiaries (or classes of beneficiaries); any natural person exercising effective ultimate control over the trust through any control / ownership chain. Trustees must disclose their status when establishing a relationship or carrying out an occasional transaction, and must preserve information about regulated agents and service providers (advisors, managers, accountants, tax advisors) for at least 15 years after the cessation of the relationship. Instr. Arts. 21, 21-A Reform 2

Refresh policy — Art. 23 statutory framework

Instr. Art. 23 sets the framework for periodic update of customer / counterparty data: items by their nature subject to change include address, telephone, economic activity, source of funds, shareholding composition. Update channels accepted: postal correspondence to last declared address, recorded phone call, validated information-technology means. The customer is independently obliged to inform the Reporting Entity of any change.

Statutory deadlines per Art. 23
  • Standard customers: periodic update per Reporting Entity's own manual; customer may self-confirm "no change" via accepted channel
  • High-risk customers: updates required at any time — no fixed scheduled cadence; reactive to risk signals
  • Reclassification trigger: if a customer is newly classified as high-risk and their data have not been updated for more than 1 year, full update must be completed within 60 calendar days of the reclassification
  • Profile inconsistency trigger: entities may require updates outside their scheduled cadence whenever an operation is inconsistent with the customer profile
Instr. Art. 23
Out-of-cycle refresh triggers (industry practice)

FATF R.10 informs the risk-based approach reflected in Instr. Arts. 10, 14 and 23 (statute does not import FATF directly; alignment is by parallel design)

  • Change in ownership ≥10%
  • Change in authorised signatories
  • Trust party change / nominee change
  • Beneficial owner becomes a PEP
  • Sanctions-list update naming a related party
  • Material deviation from declared activity profile
  • Cross-border counterparty in newly EDD-listed jurisdiction
Scheduled refresh cadence is set in the entity's AML Policy under the risk-based approach (Instr. Art. 23 — periodic update + immediate update on reclassification to high-risk within 60 calendar days).

Common citation error. The "15 business days" deadline that some manuals attach to BO updates is in fact Instr. Art. 3 (the Reporting Entity's own UIF-registration data update — own corporate data, CO appointment, etc.), not Art. 23. Customer / BO data updates follow the Art. 23 framework above (60 calendar days for high-risk reclassification; "at any time" for ongoing high-risk; periodic per own manual for standard).

Travel Rule

Per-transfer data — Travel Rule for DASPs

UIF Acuerdo 734/2022 § V «Obligations of VASPs» explicitly imports the Travel Rule into the DASP regime, citing the chain: LCLDA Art. 13 rel. Art. 72 no. 7 LOFGR rel. Instr. Art. 52 · FATF R.16. The operational data-set (7 mandatory fields + batch-processing rule + $0 floor) lives in Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 (Reform 2); the records foundation for DASPs is in Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(e) (Reform 1) (origin and destination of virtual-asset transactions). Acuerdo 734 § V is the bridge that makes Art. 52 par. 2 directly applicable to DASPs — no analogy needed.

The data set — 7 mandatory fields

The statute lists exactly seven items. The text does not distinguish between natural persons and legal entities — the same fields apply to both, only the format of identifiers differs. The matrix below shows what each field looks like for each combination.

Statutory item Natural-person originator / beneficiary Legal-entity originator / beneficiary
(a) Name of the originating customer Full legal name as on government ID Corporate name (denominación social) as registered
(b) Account number of the originating customer Bank account number / wallet account ID Same — corporate bank account or platform account ID
(c) Type and number of identification document of the originating customer DUI · passport · residence card NIT · Mercantile Registry number (matrícula)
(d) Name of the beneficiary of the transfer Full legal name as declared by the originator Corporate name as declared by the originator
(e) Account number of the beneficiary of the transfer Beneficiary's bank account / wallet ID Same — corporate bank account or platform account ID
(f) Name of the bank administering the beneficiary's account Bank or VASP holding the beneficiary's account. Cannot be populated for an unhosted (self-hosted) wallet — document the limitation and apply risk-based EDD.
(g) Transaction identification number Internal reference number, on-chain transaction hash, or unique platform identifier

Note what the statute does not require: beneficiary's identification document, beneficiary's address, originator's address, transaction date, asset type / quantity, exchange rate, sending or receiving on-chain addresses. These are operationally collected by most DASPs (for KYT, sanctions screening, accounting), but they are not Travel Rule fields under Art. 52 par. 2. Adding fields beyond the statutory minimum is a risk-based decision documented in the AML manual.

Batch processing. When several individual electronic transfers from a single originator are grouped in one batch file for transmission to multiple beneficiaries, the file must contain the required and accurate originator information and the complete beneficiary information for each transfer, under the systems determined by UIF. Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 final

Operational obligations by VASP role

A DASP can occupy three roles on the same transfer chain — sometimes simultaneously. Each role triggers its own duties at the moment the transfer enters the order book.

Originating DASP
  1. Verify originator (CDD if triggered, sanctions screen always)
  2. Attribute originating wallet to the verified client
  3. Collect Travel Rule data set
  4. Screen beneficiary against UNSC + UIF national lists
  5. Transmit data securely to beneficiary VASP
Beneficiary DASP
  1. Verify beneficiary (CDD if triggered)
  2. Attribute beneficiary wallet to the verified client
  3. Receive and validate Travel Rule data
  4. Flag missing fields; hold transfer pending data
  5. Screen originator against UNSC + UIF national lists
  6. Retain and monitor; STR if pattern emerges
Intermediary DASP
  1. Pass Travel Rule data unchanged forward
  2. Validate completeness on receipt
  3. Refuse to forward if fields missing
  4. Screen both originator and beneficiary
  5. Apply transaction monitoring at the routing point

Wallet types — hosted vs unhosted (self-hosted)

Salvadoran statute (LCLDA / Instructivo / RPSAD / LEAD) does not define hosted vs unhosted wallets. The distinction comes from FATF R.16 operational guidance and is the global industry standard for Travel Rule implementation. Both wallet types fall under the same Travel Rule (Acuerdo 734 § V → Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 + Art. 84-B(1)(e)) — but the operational handling differs.

Hosted wallet (custodial)

Counterparty is a regulated VASP holding keys on the client's behalf

  • Counterparty data received from / transmitted to the counterparty VASP via secure channel (Travel Rule protocol)
  • Counterparty VASP is identifiable; due-diligence on the VASP itself is operationally feasible
  • Reciprocal Travel Rule data exchange expected on every transfer
  • Standard CDD on the client; sanctions screen on both parties
Specific message-protocol implementation (TRP, OpenVASP, IVMS-101, etc.) is at the entity's discretion — statute prescribes the data set, not the transport.
Unhosted / self-hosted wallet

Counterparty key is held by the end-user themselves (no intermediary VASP)

  • No counterparty VASP exists to transmit Travel Rule data — the client self-attests beneficiary information
  • Client must declare beneficiary name + identifier; receiving address is the wallet itself
  • No statutory exemption in El Salvador — the Travel Rule applies in full (Acuerdo 734 § V → Art. 52 par. 2 + Art. 84-B(1)(e))
  • Wallet attribution required: prove the client controls the receiving wallet (specific method — signed-message / micro-deposit / chain-analytics attribution — at entity's discretion)
  • Apply risk-based EDD with documented rationale on file (per FATF R.16 guidance)
  • Repeated unhosted transfers → relationship review under risk-based approach
Document the attribution method in the AML manual; apply consistently; produce an audit trail per transfer.

Wallet attribution is the operational pinch point. The Travel Rule (Acuerdo 734 § V → Art. 52 par. 2 + Art. 84-B(1)(e)) requires originator and beneficiary identity but does not specify a wallet-attribution method. Industry practice (signed-message attestation, micro-deposit, KYT-vendor probabilistic attribution) is acceptable provided the method is documented in the AML manual, applied consistently, and produces an audit trail. Unhosted-wallet transfers are not exempted — they trigger risk-based EDD with documented rationale even if all other CDD factors are low-risk.

Freeze procedure on UNSC / national hit

Steps 1–3 · Immediate
  1. Confirm the match (not a false positive after basic verification). Record source, timestamp, evidence.
  2. Hold all transactions involving the matched person — funds, wallets, pending operations. Do not execute until FGR (Office of the Attorney General) issues instructions. LECAT Art. 37
  3. Report to FGR via UIF (goAML) without any delay. FGR has up to 24 hours to respond with the formal block / unblock order; the freeze itself is ordered by FGR.
Steps 4–5 · Discipline
  1. No tipping-off — do not explain the hold; do not return funds without UIF / judicial authorisation. LCLDA Art. 44
  2. Cooperate with UIF / FGR information requests; retain full file 15 years.
If the match is on a related party (not the direct client), apply same procedure plus immediate refresh of the BO chain to determine actual exposure.

Restricted jurisdictions list. Maintain an internal Restricted Jurisdictions list as part of the Risk Management Policy. The model used by the Decision Wizard combines: (i) FATF Call for Action (DPRK, Iran, Myanmar) and UNSC-sanctioned states → geofence + reject; Beyond UNSC + UIF national lists (which are statutorily binding under LECAT Art. 37), the following are commonly added by the entity's risk-management policy as commercial-risk exclusions: (ii) OFAC-comprehensive countries (Cuba, Crimea / DNR / LNR Ukraine) → geofence + reject; (iii) crypto-banned countries (China, Bangladesh, Egypt, Morocco, Algeria, Nepal, Oman, Qatar, Tunisia) → geofence + reject; (iv) FATF Grey List + partial-sanctions countries → EDD + senior approval.

Two LECAT Art. 37 reporting tracks — distinguish them.

  • Freeze track (UNSC / national list match): inform UIF / FGR without any delay on detection; hold all related operations; FGR issues block / withhold / allow-flow instructions within max 24 hours; maintain freeze until FGR or judge orders otherwise.
  • Suspicion track (operations suspected of terrorism financing): separate written report to FGR via UIF within 3 days, including attempted operations. This is independent of the freeze track and runs in parallel where applicable.

Unusual & suspicious transactions

Distinguish unusual from suspicious — the file shows the difference

Unusual operations are deviations from profile or market practice — they trigger analysis, not necessarily a report. Suspicious operations involve reasonable indicia of laundering, terrorism financing or weapons-proliferation financing — they trigger the STR pipeline. The CO is the gatekeeper; the file shows the path.

Unusual operation (operación inusual)

Deviates from the customer's declared profile or normal market practice but does not yet rise to suspicion.

Action: open analysis ticket, request explanation, refresh CDD. Document outcome — close with rationale or escalate to suspicious.

Analyst level → CO informed via daily / weekly ticket roll-up. Instr. Arts. 36, 41, 42
Suspicious operation (operación sospechosa)

Reasonable indicia that funds may relate to laundering, terrorism financing, weapons-proliferation financing, or any predicate offence — irrespective of amount.

Action: CO determination triggers the 24-h STR pipeline (next section). Tipping-off prohibited at every stage.

CO determines; clock starts at signed determination. LCLDA Art. 24

Tipping-off — operational red line. No disclosure to the client, counterparty, family, third party, or media about: existence of analysis · filing or non-filing of an STR · UIF requests · freezes · investigation · the AML reason for any account closure. Decline or delay with a neutral ground ("documentation pending") and never mention analysis or reporting. LCLDA Art. 44

Suspicious transaction reports

From red flag to STR — the 5-stage flow

Every STR follows the same five stages. Internal analysis runs up to 15 business days, extendable once by request. The filing window is 24 hours from the CO's signed determination — wall-clock, including weekends.

1
Alert opened
KYT alert / staff escalation / pattern detected. Timestamped record created in the CO queue. Source, evidence and trigger logged immediately.
2
Internal analysis — ≤ 15 business days LCLDA Art. 24
CO reviews facts, compares with profile, runs KYT and blockchain trace, checks counterparties, documents conclusions. Extension allowed once for the same period upon prior request to UIF.
3
Determination — signed and timestamped
CO records: "Suspicion confirmed [or not] — at [ISO timestamp] — by [CO name]". This is the legal trigger for the 24-h clock. Without a recorded timestamp the clock is ambiguous and UIF reads it against the entity.
4
STR filed — 24 hours wall-clock LCLDA Art. 24
File via goAML with the CDD file, Travel Rule data set, KYT report, supporting documents. Report must be timely, complete and concrete.
5
Post-filing
Relationship decision (enhanced monitoring or neutral-ground discontinuation — never cite STR as reason). Tipping-off prohibited. Cooperate with UIF / FGR information requests. Retain full file 15 years.

Attempted operations. If a client attempts but does not consummate a suspicious operation, file an attempted-operation STR through the same pipeline. The Instructivo gives 5 business days from determination of suspicion Instr. Art. 47; the LCLDA 24-h rule remains the conservative posture (see conflict callout below).

Conflict of norms — STR filing deadline. LCLDA Art. 24 sets a 24-hour filing window from determination. Instr. Art. 43 gives 5 business days for the same step. The statute (LCLDA) prevails by hierarchy. Operational posture: file within 24 h, log the determination timestamp signed by the CO, and treat the Instructivo's 5-bd as a maximum outer envelope only. Same conflict appears for attempted operations (Instr. Art. 47 = 5 bd).

STR insulation — no liability for the Reporting Entity. The STR is not considered a criminal complaint, requires no formalities of such a proceeding, and entails no criminal or civil liability against the Reporting Entity, its representatives, or employees. Instr. Art. 44

STR confidentiality and use

  • The STR is confidential, has no probative value, is used exclusively for intelligence purposes, and is not incorporated into administrative or judicial files LCLDA Art. 24
  • The amount of the operation is irrelevant for the purposes of STR LCLDA Art. 24
  • STR + voluntary STR + STR for press-news-derived information all go through the same channel Instr. Arts. 48, 49, 50

The Reporting Entity must define and maintain its own red-flag set tailored to its specific business model, products, customer segments and geographic footprint. The catalogue above is a baseline; the AML manual must include the entity's particular red flags, the alert thresholds, the analyst routing, and the periodic effectiveness review (Art. 38 last paragraph: computer systems must automatically and timely generate alerts on profile deviations). "Not every unusual transaction is suspicious" Instr. Art. 41 last sentence. Red flags trigger analysis, not automatic STR. Records & retention

15 years — every AML record, accessible to UIF

All AML records — CDD files, transactions, analyses, STRs, sanctions hits, training, audits, committee minutes — retained 15 years from the operation date or from the termination of the commercial relationship. Format: physical, digital, or electronic.

What to retain
  • CDD file per client: ID, address, occupation, BO chart, SoF, PEP screening, risk scoring
  • Travel Rule records: data per transfer, batch files, transmission logs
  • KYT logs: blockchain traces, alerts, dispositions
  • Sanctions screening: every hit, match record, decision rationale
  • STR analysis files: alert through determination through filing
  • Reports filed: STR, Operación Regulada, monthly Art. 52, freeze reports
  • Training records: attendance, evaluation, dated materials, CO sign-off
  • Audits: internal + external AML, cyber, financial
  • Committee minutes: Prevention Committee + Board AML
How to retain
  • Format: physical, digital or electronic — equally valid
  • Organisation: per client; per transaction within client; chronological within type
  • Accessibility: retrievable on UIF / CNAD request without delay
  • Integrity: tamper-evident storage; version-control on policy documents
  • Backups: secure, geo-redundant, encryption at rest
  • Access control: role-based, audit-logged, principle of least privilege
  • Disposal: only after the 15-year period expires AND no live investigation; documented destruction
Wind-down: on relationship end, retention clock starts from last operation. On corporate dissolution, records remain subject to the 15-year rule via custodian. LCLDA Art. 26

Frequently asked

Compliance FAQ

What is the difference between Art. 52 monthly reports (FI-only) and Acuerdo 734 DASP reports?
Two completely separate regimes — different subjects, different obligations.
  • Instructivo Art. 52(a)(b)(c) binds financial institutions only (banks, EMIs, brokers, SSF-supervised entities). Travel Rule + Operación Regulada thresholds — e-transfer ≥ $1,000 / family remittances ≥ $200 (FI track). Does NOT apply to DASPs. Confirmed by UIF FAQ on Reportería de Operaciones Reguladas.
  • UIF Acuerdo 734/2022 — separate UIF instrument, in force since 2 December 2022, binds DASPs specifically. Establishes DASP-only obligations: monthly client report (active/inactive/disconnected), individual transactions ≥ $1,000 within 5 bd, monthly zero-transaction notification, 8 transaction-type forms, plus the DDC threshold cascade ($1K/$4K).

The two regimes coexist: Art. 52 = FI track; Acuerdo 734 = DASP track. Reform 1 to the Instructivo (Sept 2023) added Capítulo IV-B (Arts. 84-A/B/C) with DASP Reports scope (STR + Op. Regulada per Art. 84-C); Acuerdo 734 remains in force as the operational implementation guide and was not derogated by Reform 1.

Sources: UIF Acuerdo 734/2022 — Guía PSAD-LDA-FT-ADM · UIF FAQ Operaciones Reguladas · UIF Boletín Semestral 2025 · Acuerdo 476/2023 Reform 1 · EY/Lexology analyses confirming continued vigencia.

Is the $1,000 CDD trigger for DASPs binding?
Yes — by both statutory chain and operational necessity. Statutory chain: LCLDA Art. 15(b) anchors the CDD trigger to the applicable reporting threshold for the relevant transaction type. For e-transfers that threshold is $1,000 per Instr. Art. 52(a)(b); for cash $10K per Art. 51; for other means $25K per Art. 51. Operational necessity: the $1,000 e-transfer report itself requires originator name / ID / account + beneficiary name / account / bank + transaction ID — fields that cannot be populated without CDD on the customer. So even setting the statutory chain aside, the $1,000 reporting obligation mechanically requires CDD at the same amount. Internal AML policy may calibrate stricter thresholds under FATF R.10. STR-related suspicion triggers full CDD irrespective of amount.
Why does the Travel Rule apply at any amount but Standard CDD only above $1,000?
Two different obligations. Travel Rule (Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 Reform 2) requires originator + beneficiary fields on every electronic transfer regardless of amount — the Spanish CAPS text explicitly says "even those that do not exceed the established thresholds". Standard CDD (LCLDA Art. 15(b)) is triggered when the amount crosses the relevant reporting threshold for the transaction type ($1,000 e-transfer / $10K cash / $25K other). Below $1,000 e-transfer with no risk factor: Simplified DD applies (LCLDA Art. 17 / Instr. Art. 14) — identification with deferred verification, reduced monitoring — plus Travel Rule data + sanctions screen on every transfer regardless.
Where is the Travel Rule statutory anchor for VASPs?
Two anchors layered together: (i) Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(e) — Reform 1 establishes that VASPs must "maintain customer and operation records that allow knowledge of the origin and destination of virtual-asset transactions"; (ii) Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 — Reform 2 sets out the actual data set and the no-threshold rule (financial institutions guarantee that all electronic transfers are accompanied by the required information, even those not exceeding the established thresholds).
What is the difference between an STR clock (24h) and an Operación Regulada clock (5 bd)?
Different triggers, different clocks. STR fires on a CO determination of suspicion — 24 hours wall-clock from the signed determination, including weekends, per LCLDA Art. 24. Operación Regulada fires on amount thresholds (cash > $10K or other means > $25K) — 5 business days. Both can co-exist on the same transaction (large + suspicious): file both. Note: Instructivo Art. 43 says 5 bd for STR — that conflicts with LCLDA 24h. The statute prevails by hierarchy; conservative posture is to file within 24 h.
Where is tipping-off prohibited — LECAT or LCLDA?
LCLDA Art. 44 ("Improper Disclosure of Information") is the tipping-off provision. LECAT Art. 17 in the current text is about port and maritime security offences, not tipping-off. Internal AML manuals citing "LECAT Art. 17 tipping-off" are using outdated mapping — update to LCLDA Art. 44.
Does OFAC sanctions screening apply to a Salvadoran DASP?
Not as Salvadoran AML statute — only UNSC and UIF national lists are binding under LECAT and the Instructivo. OFAC matters through (i) banking-partner contracts, (ii) USD-corridor exposure routed via US correspondents, (iii) general commercial-risk management. Manage OFAC in commercial risk policy, not the AML body. EU and UK sanctions are similarly out-of-scope for Salvadoran AML statute but matter under contracts and risk policy.
Can a DASP serve a client from a country on the EDD jurisdictions list?
Yes, with EDD baseline + senior-management written approval. The Restricted Jurisdictions list places FATF Grey List + partial-sanctions countries in the EDD-eligible category. Apply enhanced monitoring, KYT on every transaction, full SoF/SoW, periodic re-screening. If risk appetite does not allow → decline with neutral ground (never cite jurisdiction).
Who signs the determination?
The Compliance Officer (or Alternate, if delegated). Determination must be timestamped (ISO format) and signed (digital signature acceptable). Without a signed timestamp the 24-h clock is ambiguous and UIF reads it against the entity. Default operational discipline: file same business day as determination.
How often must sanctions and PEP lists be refreshed?
Statute does not fix a re-screen frequency for sanctions / PEP — Instr. Art. 23 obliges periodic update of customer data and re-screen on any material change (sanctions list update, PEP status, jurisdiction reclassification, adverse media). Operationally: subscribe to UNSC + UIF list feeds and run per-transaction screening at execution. Internal cadence (e.g., daily/weekly automated re-screen) is fixed in the entity's AML Policy under RBA, not in statute.
What records survive a DASP wind-down?
All AML records — 15 years from the operation date or from the termination of the commercial relationship, regardless of corporate dissolution (LCLDA Art. 26). The wind-down plan must transfer or escrow records to a designated custodian. Client assets in custody: returned or transferred to another licensed DASP. Records remain accessible to UIF on request through the entire 15-year period.

Special Law against Acts of Terrorism (D-108). Source of UNSC sanctions freeze (Art. 37). Tipping-off is in LCLDA Art. 44, not in LECAT.
Operación Regulada
Threshold report under LCLDA Art. 25 + Instr. Art. 51: cash > $10K or other means > $25K (single op or monthly aggregate per client). Filed within 5 business days. Includes "transactions in digital assets" by name (LCLDA Art. 25).
PEP
Politically Exposed Person — domestic / foreign / international-organisation public function. Coverage: self + family ≤ 2nd degree + close associates. Always EDD; status persists 5 yr post-cease (LCLDA Art. 19).
SoF / SoW
Source of Funds (transaction-specific) / Source of Wealth (overall patrimony). SoF: declarative at CDD baseline, documentary at EDD. SoW: not required at CDD; documentary at EDD. PEP: both mandatory.
STR
Suspicious Transaction Report (Reporte de Operación Sospechosa). Filed to UIF within 24 hours of CO determination (LCLDA Art. 24). Confidential; no probative value; intelligence purposes only; amount irrelevant.
Travel Rule
Per-transfer originator + beneficiary data requirement on every electronic transfer regardless of amount. Anchor for DASPs: UIF Acuerdo 734/2022 § V «Obligations of VASPs» imports the Travel Rule into the DASP regime via the chain LCLDA Art. 13 rel. Art. 72 no. 7 LOFGR rel. Instr. Art. 52 · FATF R.16. Operational data-set (7 fields + batch rule + $0 floor) — Instr. Art. 52 par. 2 (Reform 2). VASP-specific records foundation — Instr. Art. 84-B(1)(e) (Reform 1).
UIF
Unidad de Investigación Financiera — Salvadoran Financial Intelligence Unit. Receives all AML reports via goAML.
UNSC sanctions
UN Security Council consolidated sanctions lists (1267, 1373, 1718, 1988, 2231 + successors). Binding via LECAT Art. 37.
